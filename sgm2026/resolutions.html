What the SGM must decide

Six resolutions — each addressing a specific failure

These are not abstract demands. Each resolution addresses a documented, specific failure of governance. The petition campaign began on 2 April — and before a single signature was collected, the committee received a formal letter inviting them to act. They did not. The formal petition has been with the committee since 11 April — 20 days with no date set and no meaningful response. Only 23 May (notice required by 9 May) is now acceptable.

01
A recorded vote of no confidence in the President's handling of the nominations

The SGM will ask members to vote — on the record — on whether they have confidence in the way the President handled the nominations process at the most recent election.

Why this matters
An independent Nominations Committee was established to review candidates fairly. Its recommendations were set aside by the General Committee — and members who raised this at the AGM were not permitted to vote on it. A recorded no confidence vote is the constitutional mechanism for members to formally register their view.
02
Resignation of committee members whose nominations were improperly reinstated

Committee members whose nominations were rejected by the independent Nominations Committee but reinstated by the General Committee are asked to resign from their positions.

Why this matters
The Nominations Committee is an independent check on the elections process. When the General Committee overrules it, the purpose of that check is nullified. Members whose positions were obtained in this way hold office without the legitimacy the process was designed to provide.
03
Fresh elections under a fair, constitutionally compliant process

New elections to be held under a process that respects the Constitution, respects the Nominations Committee, and incorporates the new eligibility requirements members have voted to introduce.

Why this matters
A committee that does not hold its seat through a fair process cannot claim to govern with the mandate of the membership. Fresh elections — conducted properly — are the only way to restore that legitimacy.
04
Independent reviews of the member expulsion and staff departures

An independent body to review the circumstances of the recent member expulsion and the circumstances of all 27 staff departures — and to report findings to the membership.

Why this matters
Twenty-seven staff members have left the Club in circumstances that have been described as distressing — several have sought legal remedy. A member has also been expelled through a process that predates the fair procedure now in the Constitution. Both matters require independent scrutiny, with findings reported to members — not managed in private by the committee.
05
Formation of a Constitutional Committee to verify and update the Club's rules

A formal Constitutional Committee to be appointed — with independent legal counsel — to produce a single, verified, consolidated Rule Book incorporating all changes since the last formally printed Constitution of January 2016.

Why this matters
The Club currently has two versions of its Constitution with no clear record of member approval for all the differences between them. Julius & Creasy have been proposed as legal counsel. Until the rules are verified, every governance decision sits on uncertain ground — and the seven AGM reforms need to be formally incorporated.
06
Legal costs from the 2025–2026 proceedings not to be borne by the Club

The legal costs arising from court proceedings between 2025 and 2026 — including those in the District Court of Colombo — are not to be paid from Club funds unless formally authorised by members at a General Meeting.

Why this matters
Some members sought legal remedy because no other avenue was available. The Club has also incurred costs defending actions brought by former staff. These costs arose from governance failures. Members have a right to know how Club funds are being spent — and to authorise any such expenditure themselves.
What has already been achieved

Seven reforms, passed by members at the AGM

The six SGM resolutions build on reforms already won. At the Annual General Meeting of 28 March 2026, members voted to pass seven governance changes. These carry the force of a majority member vote and are in force from the date of passing — the committee is bound to comply.

They have not yet been formally incorporated into the written Constitution — that requires a properly convened SGM. A constitutional revision SGM is being planned for 30 September 2026, which will formally write these reforms into the Club's rules and produce the first published rule book since January 2016.

Seven reforms passed — 28 March 2026
In force from the date of passing. Formal incorporation into the Constitution planned for 30 September 2026.
  • GC minutes finalised within 14 days and shared with all members within 30 days
  • No two family members may sit on the GC, Audit Committee, Trustees, or Disciplinary Committee simultaneously
  • Term limits: maximum two consecutive years in one position; four years total; two-year hiatus required
  • Eligibility raised: President & Vice-President require 10 years' membership; General Secretary, 5 years
  • Member expulsion must go through a five-person Disciplinary Committee — including one representative chosen by the member under charge
  • The same independent process now applies to member suspension
  • Membership applications to be posted publicly with names of applicant, proposer, and seconder
